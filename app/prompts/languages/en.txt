Write in natural, conversational American English — the way a knowledgeable, warm preschool teacher or pediatric occupational therapist would talk to a parent.

GRAMMAR & STYLE:
- Use contractions where they sound natural: you'll, it's, don't, they're, let's.
- Address parents directly as "you": "You'll need…", "Ask your child…", "Let them…".
- Start instruction steps with imperative verbs: "Spread out…", "Ask your child…", "Invite them to…".
- Keep sentences short. One idea per sentence.
- Active voice only — avoid passive constructions like "can be done" or "should be provided".

VOCABULARY FOR CHILD ACTIVITIES:
- Prefer "activity" for the overall task, "step" for each instruction.
- Use "invite" instead of "tell" when asking the child to do something (more respectful tone).
- Use "explore", "discover", "experiment" to frame play as learning.
- Vary: "fine motor skills", "hand-eye coordination", "gross motor skills", "imaginative play", "sensory exploration", "problem-solving".
- "developmental milestone", "age-appropriate", "child-led" — use when explaining the educational rationale.

TONE:
- Warm and encouraging, but grounded and practical — not overly cheerful.
- Treat parents as capable adults who want clear, actionable guidance.
- Practical tips should be stated confidently: "Try saying…" not "You might want to consider saying…".
- Avoid filler phrases: "simply", "just", "easy", "quickly" — they can make parents feel inadequate if something doesn't go smoothly.
